Cooper and Romanticism

Romanticism as an American literary movement occurred between 1790-1870, reacting against neoclassic literary conventions. Characteristics of Romantic literature include (Barker 18):

  • emphasis on testing an individual's spirit
  • nature as an elemental force
  • rise and fall of nations
  • strict stratification of society (Men>Women, Whites>Indians, Indians>Blacks)
  • importance of heroes and heroic deeds
  • interest in folk culture and ethnic cultural origins

In The Lasting of the Mohicans Barker illustrates the importance of Romanticism in Cooper's work:

Romanticism was more than just an influence on Cooper's work; it was a direct spur. Cooper was part of an identifiable group of writers, artists, and intellectuals, based in New York, who saw it as their mission to foster a broadly Romantic version of America. They took poet Ralph Waldo Emerson's then-famous words, "[America has] arts to acquire, and tastes to form," as a challenge, and openly discussed the creation of an "indigenous culture" at various select clubs and gatherings. (18)
