introduction

Dawson's Landing is a place governed by a combination of old-world aristocracy and capitalist democracy. As a river-front community catering to steamboat traffic the town is fronted by "The main street, one block back from the river and running parallel with it, [which was] the sole business street." But the commerce of this street is not enough to sustain the economy of a town. In fact, "Dawson's Landing was a slaveholding town, with a rich, slaveworked grain and pork country back of it. The town was sleepy and comfortable and contented. It was fifty years old, and was growing slowly--very slowly, in fact, but it was still growing." In other words, the less visible, yet more powerful economy of Dawson's landing was rooted in plantations set back from the river, the "grain and pork country." This is a slave economy.

But since Dawson's Landing is located " half a days journey, per steamboat, below St. Louis" slave life was not as severe as in the lower South, or "down the river". As Malcom Bradbury writes in the Penguin Classic Edition "the slaveholding economy [in Twain's location of the town] and the dominance of the families who have moved in from Virginia can be much more stressed; and hence the town has a much more feudal and much less northern air" (p.317). Since Dawson's Landing is located approximately halfway between St. Louis and Cairo (where harsh agricultural slavery starts on the river, according to Huckleberry Finn), slavery in Dawson's Landing is mostly domestic and governed by gentlemanly codes of Old Dominion behavior. These codes, which encouraged the humane (and almost human) treatment of slaves, give rise to one of the main legal dilemmas of Twain's novel: if a slave is a piece of property, how can he be found guilty of a human crime like murder?
