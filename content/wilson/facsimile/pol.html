Politics

Politics plays a powerful role in the shaping of identity in the town of Dawson's Landing both in obvious and subtle ways. In the literal sense, the political process is manifest in the various official elections staged throughout the novel. These elections decide who is popular and powerful, and who is ruined. But informal politics play a large role in deciding the fate and identity of the characters. David Wilson can only be what the town says he is (a pudd'nhead or a hero), and he must conform in order to be accepted. The twins are initially heralded by consensus fo the twonspeople, yet are destroyed by a rumor.

  • Twian's depiction of mob politics initially portrays David Wilson'snaming as a Pudd'nhead as a democratic process.
  • At the thought of the twins' appearance, Rowena exclaims "they're ours," as if through contact with Angelo and Luigi her political status would increase. Yet as the story progresses, Wilson and the Twins switch status as a result of public opinion.
  • When Tom Driscoll suggests to David Wilson and the hired detective that the knife belonging to the twins might still be in their possession, he starts a rumor which eventually has political impact upon the twins' reputation. As a result they are discredited during Percy Driscoll's speech.
  • The economic status and ancestral lineage of the F.F.V. families have a profound impact upon their status in democratic poitics.
  • David Wilson's christening as a "pudd'nhead" is portrayed as an election.
  • Courtroom scenes of David Wilson are dramatized as a mixture of theatre and popular democratic appeal. In these scenes, Wilson plays to the mob, as much a politition and performer as an attorney. The courtroom spectators are portrayed as a performance audience and political body to be swayed through a manipulation of their prejudices. Twain thus conflates the triad of entertainment performance, politics, and democratic law.
