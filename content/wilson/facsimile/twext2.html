introduction		 Politics

When the budding attorney David Wilson arrives in Dawson's Landing he is democratically elected a "Pudd'nhead" because of his ironic comment about "half a dog." After he makes is comment about the disposal of the yelping dog, his statement is discussed among his new neighbors. He is discussed as "a lummox," or "a labric--just a Simon-pure labrick, if there ever was one," then as "a dam fool," and "a perfect jackass," though "it ain't too far to say he is a pudd'nhead."

Thereby "Mr. Wilson stood elected. The incident was told all over the town, and gravely [ as well as democratically] discussed by everybody."

Because this election usurps Wilson's status as a keeper of the law, Twain seems to suggests that the process of public opinion shapes not only individuals' careers, but the maintenance of legal systems in the old South.
