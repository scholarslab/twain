Race
  • Pudd'nhead Wilson explores the idea of miscegenation through the characters Tom and Roxy. Both Tom and Roxy's slave status are the result of the sexual exploitation of slave women by members of the F.F.V. aristocracy. Through these characters, Twain calls into question the fractional nature of the color line; Roxy is one-sixteenth black, and Tom is one- thritysecond black, yet both are fully slaves. As David Wilson observes, in the South killing half a dog produces a whole dead dog, just as a "teaspoon of black blood" results in social incrimination and a life of misery.
  • Besides applying the obvious meanings of race in Pudd'nhead Wilson which directly pertain to slavery and Tom Driscoll's and Roxy's partial African-American lineage, Twain toys with nineteenth-century racial stereotypes.
  • The reader first comes into contact with Roxy as Wilson overhears her banter with Jasper. Her voice fits the "minstrel show" stereotype: "Clah to goodness if dat conceit o' yo'n strikes in, Jasper, it gwine to kill you sho.'" The reader hears Roxy's black voice before seeing her white features, so casts her as a black slave.
  • Such a depiction contrasts with Twain's physical description of Roxy as "noble" and "stately." Through this contradiction, Twain confuses and complicates the reader's stereoptyes of African Americans.
  • Twain subtly manipulates the theme of race as a dialectic between appearance and essence. He describes the two babies, the aristocratic real Tom Driscoll as visually indistinguishable from Valet de Chambers, who is a slave. He also discusses the markings of the striped pole which symbolizes European nobility as the marker of the humble barber shop in America; accepted visual standards of status become irrelevant.
  • This striped-pole confusion of appearances is linked to the miscegenation which caused Roxy's and the false Tom's mixed race. The F.F.V. responsible appear as noble symbols of a pure European race, although their acts produce a humbled offspring. Twain exploits this irony in portraying the town's distress over a series of robberys, yet their acceptance the F>F>V>'s theft of slaves freedom, and their virtual denial of forced miscengenation.
