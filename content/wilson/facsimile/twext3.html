introduction
Twain questions symbols of lineage, the cornerstone of aristocratic rule, when he writes in his introduction of the town. He writes: "the candy-striped pole which indicated nobility proud and ancient along palace-bordered canals of Venice indicated merely the humble barber-shop along the main street of Dawson's landing." In the old-world the striped pole signifies nobility. It is humbled in the new world.

As Twain describes the town's "chief citizen" he depicts the Virginia gentleman as the self-conscious protector of status: "The chief citizen [of Dawson's landing] was York Leicester Driscoll, about forty years old, judge of the county court. He was very proud of his old Virginia ancestry, and in his hospitalities and his rather formal and stately manners he kept up its traditions. He was fine and just and generous. To be a gentleman--a gentleman without stain or blemish--was his only religion, and to it he was always faithful." Yet even the town was "faithful" to Driscoll's role as feudal lord, yet this choice to follow the F.F.V. tradition came as a democratic consensus which was motivated by economics.

Since Driscoll is the ever-popular "county judge," and at the same time an old Virginia aristocrat, he embodies a conflation of the democratic attributes of law and popularity as well as feudal rule and supremacy.
