By the end of On the Plantation, Lee has surrendered, slavery has been abolished, and "the old things had passed away." The illustration here refers to the effects of Sherman's march on the population of middle Georgia: "The people that Joe saw went about with anxious faces, and even the negroes were frightened." We learn a few pages later that the many of the younger slaves had followed the Federal army away toward freedom, but even at the end of the War "some of the older negroes were in their accustomed places." The slave cabins stand empty, "but the old plantation days still live in [Joe's] dreams."
Harris, On the Plantation (1892)
